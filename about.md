# Young-jun Lee

Server Developer In Snow. I build things that shouldn't work, make them work anyway, and occasionally make legal history.

---

## The Short Version

I grew up dreaming of being a poet, then a novelist, then a filmmaker. Ended up a developer. Turns out engineering and storytelling aren't that different — both are about finding the structure underneath the chaos.

I've lived in a banziha (반지하) — the half-underground apartment made famous by the film *Parasite*. I've survived a real estate fraud. I've been tortured by an upstairs neighbor for 20 months, ended up in the emergency room with a panic attack, and decided to turn the whole thing into a Supreme Court case. Still climbing.

---

## What I Do

I'm a server developer, but that's a narrow way to put it.

At Naver Snow, I've designed LLM orchestration systems that cut response time from 90 seconds to under 10. I've built recommendation systems solo — handling data pipelines, model training, and Spring servers simultaneously. I've architected a Core Library now running across 25 internal services. I debugged Japanese text quality issues by analyzing Yahoo Japan news and tracing the problem to embedding coordinate space. I migrated a 10-year-old legacy server from 9 physical machines down to 3 VMs while handling 100K RPS push spikes.

I don't wait to be asked. I find the problem, diagnose the structure, and build the fix.

**Stack:** Kotlin · Java · Python · Spring · LLM Orchestration · RAG · Embedding Search · Kubeflow · TorchServe · Redis · Coroutines · Docker · Kubernetes

## The Lawsuit

In February 2026, after 20 months of floor noise, a panic attack, and an ER visit — I decided not to just survive it. I'm building a case aimed at the Supreme Court of Korea, arguing two things:

1. **The right to residence** should extend to remote workers whose home is also their workplace.
2. **The cognitive assets of knowledge workers** should be quantifiable as legal damages.

140+ pieces of evidence. Git-managed. Timestamped. Still counting.

I used AI to analyze case law, structure legal arguments, and design the evidence system. This is what AI looks like in the real world.
